Three languages

Three languages. Most founders are reading the wrong one.

Finance is the story. Accounting is the record. Cash is the truth.

Every founder is reading three financial languages at once. Nobody told you they were different. That’s not a literacy problem. It’s how the system is set up. You learn the differences the hard way, in the wrong order, when something stops adding up.

01Finance

The story.

What your business is supposed to do. The plan. The forecast. The version of the future you tell investors, staff, and yourself at 11pm when sleep isn’t coming.

02Accounting

The record.

What already happened, organised for compliance. Your P&L. Your balance sheet. Your BAS. Accurate, but built for the ATO. Not for the decision you have to make on Thursday morning.

03Cash

The truth.

What’s actually moving through the bank. When it arrives. When it leaves. What’s left to absorb a surprise. Cash doesn’t care about accrual rules. Cash moves when it moves.

Most decisions get made in Finance, recorded in Accounting, and paid for in Cash. When those three languages disagree, the founder feels it long before the reports show it.

Nine patterns

Nine patterns we see in cash-stressed businesses.

These aren’t theories. They’re the patterns the little birdie watches for, named, every week, in businesses that look profitable from the outside. Most have three or four running at once. The Birdie Score tells you which ones are yours.

01

Phantom Revenue

Money on the report that hasn’t arrived in the bank. Platform holdbacks, timing gaps, invoices waiting to clear.

02

Cost Overhang

Fixed costs that grew with the business and never got tested against the cash cycle. The monthly nut is higher than the founder thinks.

03

Structural Bleed

Cash leaking between entities, accounts, or platforms through transfers no one wrote down. One part of the business quietly funding another.

04

Decision Drift

Decisions made on gut feel because the data is too old, too unclear, or too painful to look at properly.

05

Silent Liability

Obligations stacking up in the background that standard reports don’t surface. PAYG. GST. Super accrued. Leave entitlements. Quiet, then loud.

06

Blind Position

You can’t answer basic questions about your own financial position with confidence. True margin. Cash runway. Break-even by service line.

07

Growth Trap

Revenue rising faster than the financial infrastructure beneath it can carry. Winning more work makes the cash problem worse, not better.

08

Founder Extraction

Personal and business cash flows have become tangled up. The founder is last in the queue.

09

Capital Misalignment

The wrong type of funding, at the wrong time, on the wrong terms. The capital structure doesn’t match how the business actually works.

Most founders we work with have three or four of these running at once. None of them knew before they looked.

What we see

What founders don’t realise they’re carrying.

After two decades sitting across the desk from founders, the same things go unsaid in nearly every conversation. The founder doesn’t know to ask. The accountant doesn’t know to volunteer it. The information sits there quietly, and it stays quiet until it stops being quiet.

01

The real cost of carrying your top staff.

Founders know what they pay people. They don’t always know what those people actually cost. Super, workcover, leave accruals, the hidden cost of cover when they take time off, the cost of replacing them if they leave. The number is rarely the number on the offer letter.

02

Where the business is funding the founder, and where the founder is funding the business.

Most founder-run businesses have money flowing both ways across the same line. A vehicle the business pays for. A loan that was never documented. A drawdown that became a habit. Division 7A in the background. Most founders don’t realise these are intertwined until someone draws the diagram.

03

The tax position you’ve already triggered but haven’t paid for yet.

Sales already made. Stock already moved. Plant and equipment that depreciated faster than the book says. The tax outcome of the year is usually decided by August. Most founders only find out in May.

04

What your business is actually worth, separate from what it earns.

Earnings and value aren’t the same number. The business with strong systems and a thin founder is worth more than the business with strong earnings and a founder who’s the system. Most founders don’t know which one they’ve built.

05

The deals you’ve signed that quietly compound against you.

Personal guarantees on leases. Director loans on the wrong side of the balance sheet. Rolling supplier terms that tightened during a tough month and never loosened back. The contracts you signed years ago are still working, and not always in your favour.

06

The cost of decisions you’re making without seeing the full picture.

Hiring on gut. Pricing on intuition. Discounting to win the deal. Carrying a customer who’s quietly losing you money. Each one is small. Each one is rational in isolation. Stacked together, they’re the difference between a business that compounds and a business that treads water.

None of this is failure. It’s what happens when you’ve been running fast and nobody’s been pointed at the underneath. The Birdie Score is the first look. The phone call is where we tell you what we see.
